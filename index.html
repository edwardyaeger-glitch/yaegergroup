Psychotherapy

Psychotherapy requires more than training. It requires judgment, restraint, and the capacity to tolerate complexity without rushing to resolution.

Much of what brings people into therapy is not immediately dramatic or diagnosable. It is quieter: a sense that familiar ways of coping no longer hold; that decisions feel heavier than they should; that roles once inhabited with ease now require effort or performance.

The aim is not to optimize or perform wellness, but to understand what is happening, why it is happening, and how to respond in a way that can be sustained and lived with over time.

My clinical work is grounded in licensed training and conducted within ethical and professional boundaries. I am attentive to role clarity and do not blur psychotherapy with advisory, consulting, or performance-oriented work. Its purpose is psychological health, clarity, and capacity.

What differentiates my work is not a particular technique or orientation, but the way responsibility is held. I attend closely to context (personal, relational, cultural, and professional) without collapsing the work into problem-solving or reassurance.

I take an interactive, integrative, and eclectic approach: drawing from an array of applied theories, modalities, and approaches rather than from one singular school of thought. Approaches include Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, Existential, Narrative, Attachment-Based, Dialectical, Family Systems, and Psychodynamic, among others.

I work deliberately and selectively. Not every concern requires therapy, and not every therapeutic approach is a good fit.

Business & Organizational Consulting

There are moments when something important is not working, yet nothing is ostensibly “broken.” Decisions drag. Conversations repeat. People perform competence while carefully avoiding what actually needs to be addressed. The organization keeps moving, but judgment erodes and trust ebbs. Everyone feels it, but no one quite names it, primarily out of fear.

For most people in an organization, guidance exists. Managers look to senior managers. Senior managers look to executives, who draw on boards of directors, management consultants, attorneys, and, when necessary, public-facing counsel. In many situations, that ecosystem is sufficient.

The work I provide exists for the moments when it is not.

When the issue is not strategy, legality, optics, or execution, but how power is being exercised, how responsibility is being carried, or how avoidance has quietly taken hold, traditional guidance often stops short. Not because it is wrong, but because it is not designed for that layer of reality.

I work privately and almost entirely by referral. The work is not standardized, not public-facing, and not designed to scale; it involves direct conversations about judgment, consequence, and responsibility, handled carefully, without sycophancy, flattery, or agenda.

I am brought in to provide independent judgment and psychological containment when existing structures are no longer sufficient to do either.

In plain terms, I help surface what is interfering with judgment, strip away the fear and misattribution surrounding it, and support leaders in dealing with it before the cost compounds further.

Executive-Level Consulting

Most management consultancies focus on strategy, technology, scale, and efficiency. Even those grounded in industrial/organizational psychology have historically deprioritized the human dimension, as revenue models tended to favor long-term retainers and incremental upselling.

In the post–Big Data era, and especially with the rise of AI, the true competitive differentiator will be how organizations understand, support, and engage people. Especially their own. Human judgment, creativity, imagination, and meaning-making cannot be automated.

Consulting Focus

I work with executive teams and senior leaders on:

  • Organizational culture and psychological climate
  • Leadership behavior and emotional impact
  • Burnout, disengagement, and talent erosion
  • Identity, power, and role complexity at senior levels
  • Change management and human response to disruption
  • The psychological implications of AI and technological acceleration

This work prioritizes clarity, containment, and sustainability. Not performative culture initiatives, baseless trends, or abstract frameworks.

This is not HR consulting. It is executive-level work on how people actually think, feel, relate, and perform inside complex organizations.

Advisory

Advisory is a structured, goal-oriented engagement focused on career, vocation, and professional direction. It draws on the same relational depth as clinical work, but the scope is different. We are not treating a diagnosis. We are working through a challenge, building toward a specific outcome, or pressure-testing a decision.

For some clients, advisory work follows a course of therapy once clinical concerns have stabilized and the remaining focus is professional. For others, advisory is the starting point. Their needs were never clinical to begin with.

How Advisory Differs from Therapy

Where psychotherapy is ongoing, open-ended, and organized around a clinical treatment plan, advisory is goal-oriented and often time-bounded or project-based. It is structured around objectives, milestones, and deliverables rather than diagnosis. Advisory is private-pay only and does not involve a clinical record, though fees may be employer-reimbursable or tax-deductible.

Who Advisory Is For

Advisory is appropriate for professionals at any stage (early career, mid-career, or senior leadership) who are working through questions that are fundamentally about their professional direction, not their mental health.

  • Professionals in transition: navigating a job change, industry shift, promotion, or return to the workforce after a leave
  • Leaders and managers: working on executive presence, team dynamics, difficult conversations, or organizational politics
  • Entrepreneurs and founders: pressure-testing business decisions, managing the psychological demands of ownership, or building strategic clarity
  • High performers under pressure: sustaining performance without burnout, managing competing demands, or recalibrating after a setback

How It Works

  1. Initial conversation. We meet to discuss your situation, define objectives, and determine whether advisory is the right format.
  2. Engagement structure. Advisory can be session-based, project-based, or a defined series. We set the cadence and scope together based on what you are working toward.
  3. Ongoing work. Sessions are focused, practical, and forward-looking. Expect direct feedback, strategic framing, and accountability.
  4. Pricing. Advisory fees are discussed individually. A separate document on tax deductibility and employer reimbursement is available upon request.

Vocational Advisory

There are moments when someone's professional life appears coherent, successful, and fully resourced, yet something essential begins to fray beneath the surface. The work continues. The decisions keep coming. The public-facing machine functions. And yet judgment dulls, motivation wanes, and choices that once felt intuitive become effortful. Nothing is visibly “broken,” but something important is no longer working in the way it once did.

I work with individuals whose professional lives are highly visible, tightly managed, and consequential: artists, performers, leaders, and public figures whose decisions ripple outward to teams, organizations, and audiences. At that level, many familiar forms of guidance stop being useful. Not because they are incompetent, but because they are structurally unsuited to the realities involved.

Keep the rigor of the therapist. Remove the emotional bias of loved ones. Account for the structural incentives that shape agency relationships. What remains is the need for an independent, trusted advisor.

I am not hired help. I am not an extension of a team. I am not accountable to anyone's internal hierarchy. I am engaged precisely because I am not embedded in it.

I do not manage logistics, messaging, or performance. I work independently, and only when independence is essential. Both yours and mine.

My vocational advisory work draws on licensed psychotherapy, professional counseling, and decades of advisory experience, not as a bundle of services, but as a way of working. The focus is not motivation or optimization, but coherence: between values and action, identity and responsibility, ambition and meaning.

This is not management coaching or motivational advising. It is structured, reflective work intended to support sound judgment and sustained orientation under pressure.

I work privately and almost entirely by referral. The work is individualized, discreet, and not designed to scale. It exists for moments when the cost of continuing as things are, quiet, cumulative, and often unnamed, has begun to outweigh the discomfort of addressing what has shifted.

Contract Advisory

Organizations often identify leaders-in-training early but lack sophisticated support structures to help those individuals evolve from effective managers into adaptive, reflective leaders.

Traditional executive coaches can offer mentorship and strategy but cannot ethically or legally provide psychotherapy. Most therapists, including those accessed through EAPs, can address mental health concerns but lack the professional exposure, leadership fluency, or organizational context to support leadership development, career navigation, and real-world complexity.

Because I am both a licensed clinician and an experienced advisor, employees receive coherent, integrated support rather than fragmented services across coaching, therapy, and HR interventions. This engagement bridges leadership development and professional identity, emotional intelligence and psychological resilience, career planning and decision-making, and stress management and mental health support.

  • High-potential employees: preparing for leadership roles
  • Managers: working through authority, visibility, or influence
  • Employees under strain: during promotion, role expansion, or organizational change
  • Retention investments: meaningful support for valued talent through periods of growth

Engagements are typically short- to medium-term retainers focused on individual development while remaining aligned with organizational goals and governance. Conversations begin by clarifying scope, goals, governance, and fit.
